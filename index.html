Space exploration

Outer space, commonly shortened to space, is the expanse that exists beyond Earth and its atmosphere and between celestial bodies. We, as humans, have always wondered what lies beyond the stars and this is a short tribute to human space exploration.

On the 4 October, 1957, the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (U.S.S.R.) launched the first artificial satellite, Sputnik 1, into space. The space race had begun between the between two Cold War adversaries, the Soviet Union and the United States, to achieve superior spaceflight capability. The first U.S. satellite, Explorer 1, went into orbit only a few months later, on the 31 January 1958. Four years later on 12 April 1961, Russian Lt. Yuri Gagarin became the first human to venture in space and orbit Earth in Vostok 1.

During the 1960s, NASA made progress toward President Kennedyâ€™s goal of landing a human on the moon with a program called Project Gemini, in which astronauts tested technology needed for future flights to the moon, and tested their own ability to endure many days in spaceflight. Project Gemini was followed by Project Apollo, which took astronauts into orbit around the moon and to the lunar surface between 1968 and 1972. In 1969, on Apollo 11 , the United States sent the first astronauts to the Moon, and Neil Armstrong became the first human to set foot on its surface. During the landed missions, astronauts collected samples of rocks and lunar dust that scientists still study to learn about the moon.
